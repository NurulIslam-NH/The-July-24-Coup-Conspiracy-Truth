Two Competing Narratives

The events of July-August 2024 are broadly understood through two conflicting frameworks. The dominant narrative portrays a spontaneous student uprising, while the analytical white paper and its supporting evidence argue for a pre-planned, coordinated operation.

Narrative A: The "Student Uprising"

A spontaneous, leaderless mass movement initiated by students protesting quota reforms, which escalated into a popular revolution against state repression, culminating in the ouster of the government.

Narrative B: The "Coordinated Operation"

A pre-planned regime change operation that used the quota protest as a "Trojan Horse." This thesis alleges coordination between student leaders, extremist groups, and foreign entities, employing deliberate violence to manufacture a crisis.

Internal Actors: A Coordinated Front

The white paper cites admissions from key figures and groups to argue that the movement was a structured coalition rather than a spontaneous event. Student leaders, Islamist political groups, and banned extremist organizations all claimed active, collaborative roles.

The analysis points to claims of involvement from student coordinators (like Nahid Islam and Hannan Masud), established political groups (like Jamaat-e-Islami/Shibir), and banned militant outfits (like Hizb ut-Tahrir), who all stated they were active participants or "co-fighters."

Organization & Planning

Evidence cited suggests a high degree of pre-planning. This includes statements from student leaders about long-term goals and contingencies for armed conflict, challenging the idea of a purely reactive movement.

"First Contact: August 1"

Strategy: Manufacturing a "Genocide" Narrative

The white paper alleges that the narrative of a "student genocide" was deliberately manufactured. This involved two key components: discrediting official data by submitting fraudulent victim lists to the UN, and using armed "sniper" cells to kill protestors and police alike to provoke a crisis.

7.62mm

Forensic Rebuttal of the UN (OHCHR) Report

A 68-page forensic rebuttal by the International Crimes Research Foundation (ICRF) directly challenges the UN OHCHR's fact-finding report, labeling it a "political weapon." The rebuttal claims the UN report relied on a flawed mandate, biased timelines, and unverified data from partisan sources.

Case Study: The Autopsy of Abu Sayed

OHCHR Report Claim (Para 101)

Asserted that "no proper autopsy, in line with international forensic standards, was conducted" for Abu Sayed.

ICRF Rebuttal (Source 2)

Directly contradicts this, citing the existence of a "formal post-mortem report prepared at Rangpur Medical College Hospital" which is part of the public and legal record.

This discrepancy is used to question the methodological rigor and impartiality of the entire OHCHR investigation, suggesting it ignored contradictory forensic evidence to fit a pre-determined narrative.

Conclusion: An Indemnified Coalition

The white paper concludes that the "Indemnity Order" passed by the interim government, which grants immunity for actions taken during the "movement," serves as final proof of a coordinated coup. It argues this order protects all members of the coalition—including student leaders, extremist groups, and armed cells—from accountability for violence and murder, cementing their control and legitimizing the "regime change."
